History

Narendra Modi was born in Vadnagar, Gujarat, on September 17, 1950, into a grocer family. He claimed to have worked in his father's tea shop as a child, though there are disputes regarding this. He completed his higher secondary education in 1967 and was known for his debating skills and interest in theatre, influencing his political image. At eight, he joined the Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh (RSS) and had mentors like Lakshmanrao Inamdar. He had an arranged but unconsummated marriage to Jashodaben, kept secret for years. Modi's early years involved extensive travel through northern and northeastern India. In 1971, he joined a Jana Sangh Satyagraha and was briefly held in Tihar Jail for supporting the Bangladesh Liberation War. After the 1971 war, he became a full-time RSS campaigner and obtained BA and MA degrees amid controversies over their authenticity. These early experiences and connections in Modi's life laid the groundwork for his later political career, eventually leading him to become the Prime Minister of India in 2014.

Beti Bachao, Beti Padhao: Caring for the Girl Child

Our Mantra should be: ‘Beta Beti, Ek Samaan’ "Let us celebrate the birth of the girl child. We should be equally proud of our daughters. I urge you to sow five plants when your daughter is born to celebrate the occasion." -PM Narendra Modi to citizens of his adopted village Jayapur. Beti Bachao Beti Padhao (BBBP) was launched by the Prime Minister on 22nd January, 2015 at Panipat, Haryana. BBBP addresses the declining Child Sex Ratio (CSR) and related issues of women empowerment over a life-cycle continuum. It is a tri-ministerial effort of Ministries of Women and Child Development, Health & Family

Demonetisation in India

Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi had said the decision to scrap high-value bank notes will give the economy a further boost, and would provide long-term benefits, including forcing the country’s vast shadow economy into the open. Under the policy, both 500 and 1,000 rupee notes ceased to be legal tenders after December 31, 2016. “We took the demonetisation decision not for some short-term windfall gain, but for a long-term structural transformation,” Modi was quoted as saying by Economic Times. Modi added the move would bring cash from the shadow economy into the mainstream. The Prime Minister had said the demonetisation action was needed to fight corruption and fraud and cut off financing for attacks by militants who target India. “The multiplier effect of the introduction of money which was, till now, uselessly hoarded and stocked away as cash into the active economic system will give the economy a further boost,” he said.

Ayodhya

Prime Minister Narendra Modi launched the construction of a Hindu temple in the northern town of Ayodhya, which is the birthplace of Lord Ram, after the Supreme Court ruled that Hindus be allowed to build a temple there, ending years of litigation. Modi, whose Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) campaigned for more than three decades for the temple, unveiled a plaque at the site in an elaborate ceremony to inaugurate construction.

Kashmir

The Modi government revoked Article 370 which gave special status to Jammu and Kashmir and proposed that the state be bifurcated into two union territories, Jammu and Kashmir and Ladakh. "The president on the recommendation of Parliament is pleased to declare as from 5th of August 2019, all clauses of the said Article 370 shall cease to be operative... ," stated the resolution moved by the Home Minister Amit Shah. The decision meant revocation of a bar on property purchases by people from outside the Indian state of Jammu and Kashmir and that state government jobs and some college places were no longer reserved for state residents.






Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act

President Ram Nath Kovind gave assent to the triple talaq bill passed by Parliament, turning it into a law which made the practice of instant divorce among Muslims a punishable offence. The law called The Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019 made talaq-e-biddat or any other similar form of talaq having the effect of instantaneous and irrevocable divorce pronounced by a Muslim husband void and illegal. It made it illegal to pronounce talaq three times in spoken, written or through SMS or WhatsApp or any other electronic chat in one sitting. "Any pronouncement of talaq by a Muslim husband upon his wife, by words, either spoken or written or in electronic form or in any other manner whatsoever, shall be void and illegal," the law says



MISSIONS

2016 Indian Line of Control strike

On September 29, 2016, Indian Army commandos conducted a cross-border raid into Pakistani-administered Kashmir, occurring in response to a militant attack on an Indian army outpost in Uri, Jammu and Kashmir, which claimed the lives of 19 soldiers. Casualty estimates from India's operation varied widely, with India claiming significant militant casualties and Pakistan acknowledging the deaths of two soldiers. Pakistan initially denied Indian troops had crossed the Line of Control, but later admitted to capturing an Indian soldier. Independent analysts criticized India's casualty figures as exaggerated and questioned the use of the term "surgical strike." The incident led to continued border clashes and casualties in the following months, with media coverage reflecting nationalistic perspectives and uncertainties surrounding the attack.

         

2019 Balakot airstrike

The 2019 Balakot airstrike involved Indian warplanes conducting a bombing raid on February 26, 2019, in Balakot, Pakistan, targeting an alleged terrorist training camp. Open-source satellite imagery later revealed that no significant targets were hit. Pakistan shot down an Indian warplane and took its pilot prisoner the next day, while an Indian helicopter was accidentally downed by anti-aircraft fire, resulting in the deaths of several airmen. India's ruling party used the airstrike to bolster its patriotic appeal during the April 2019 general elections. The airstrike marked the first time since the 1971 war that warplanes crossed the Line of Control between India and Pakistan. Subsequent investigations raised questions about the effectiveness of the strike, with no evidence of significant damage found 47 days later on the alleged target.

         



2019 Pulwama attack

On February 14, 2019, the Pulwama attack took place in the Pulwama district of the former state of Jammu and Kashmir. A suicide bomber, Adil Ahmad Dar, targeted a convoy of Indian Central Reserve Police Force (CRPF) personnel, resulting in the deaths of 40 CRPF personnel. The responsibility for the attack was claimed by the Pakistan-based Jaish-e-Mohammed terrorist group. India accused Pakistan of involvement, leading to strained relations and the 2019 India-Pakistan military standoff. Indian investigations identified 19 suspects, with some killed and others arrested. It was revealed that the Indian government had ignored multiple intelligence warnings about the attack. Satya Pal Malik, the then-governor, alleged that Prime Minister Narendra Modi asked him to stay silent about security lapses.
